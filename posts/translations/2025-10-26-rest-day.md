Mental fatigue is far more capable than physical exhaustion of making one collapse and fall easily. Especially in the AI field where I work, mental fatigue is something almost inevitable and unchangeable. Even though I have been settled in Chennai for the past five months, every now and then the southern breeze pulls me southward, like a wave retreating, or like the moon in the sky. Just as the wave surges back, my mental fatigue lifts, and I head back to Chennai! Carrying the essence of the deep south within me...

Anyway, next week I am definitely going for the <qn>Kodai</qn> festival. After the Kodai festival, if I take a dip in the <qn>Kutralam</qn> waterfalls, mental fatigue will surely vanish without a trace!

I have heard some Christian priests preach that Sunday is a day to be filled with the Spirit, and when filled with that Spirit, the remaining six days become joyful with the Spirit’s coolness. A day of rest is necessary for everyone! Look at the example of the ant shown in the Bible! It is wise to allocate some time for rest alongside sustained work. It is like a Battery Charging Day. For Seventh-day Adventists, that day is Saturday!

Beyond spirituality, what everyone needs is a day of rest! Set aside one day in the week as a rest day, fill yourself with the Spirit to cool your soul! That Spirit is like the Kutralam cascades! Or like a wonderfully cool southern breeze! Whatever brings comfort to your mind and fills you with joy, that is the Spirit!

I am pouring out all this philosophy at this midnight hour! Why!? If you wake up in the middle of the night, count your blessings! You are in the time meant for doing the impossible! (You might think: Why on earth do we have to wake up at midnight?) In my conclusion, the emptiness of mind at midnight is the reason. One day, while rushing from Chennai to Tirunelveli, I was working on my laptop right there on the bus. After three hours, mental fatigue crept in. I closed the laptop, relaxed, and slept. Later, when I woke up in the middle of the night, some new energy surged. It urged my mind to open the laptop again. This is it! This is what is making me write this piece today! What else!? It is the mind! The mind itself! But a transformed mind! A weary mind, after sleep, has become a mind seeking strength. For some reason, midnight further energizes the mind.

What am I going to write? Since I named it AriZone, this Zone has to describe Ari. Because of the immense interest Ari has in AI, AriZone should not lose its Reality (R) and turn into AiZone. That is the wordplay behind AriZone and AiZone, and that is why this sentence is deliberately written here! Let that be; let me fill the space a bit!

I will write about what happened today! I will also write about what happens in the future! Don’t think of me as Nostradamus. From now on, in the pages of my life, I intend to collect, in this small space, the interesting things that happen from time to time, as well as the unforgettable matters of the heart from before.

Even though it has been two weeks since I came to the village, I had not yet gone to the spot under the banyan tree where I usually go to rest and sleep a little! Like “Neelikku kalliye saatchi” (the cactus bears witness for <qn>Neeli</qn>), that banyan tree is witness to my past! Many days with a bag of books! Some days with <qn>fried flying termites</qn>! Many days in wet rain! Some days with boiled Eenth (Ceylon date palm)! Many days with small tears! One day with heavy weeping! The day in Panguni when, before Aiyan rose in Uttiram, the one who turned blood into milk and raised me while hiding her love, my father’s mother, left the world! That one day of terrible mental agony, with bloodshot eyes full of tears, legs refusing to move, body trembling!

“Go,” my mind finally agreed somehow. In the evening twilight, around 5 o’clock, I crossed both the lower and upper Maruva streams. Keezhamaruva and Melamaruva are their names. In childhood, during dry seasons I saw small snakes there; during rainy seasons I caught “koonipodi” (small prawns) and played. Between the two streams, in the middle, there is a slightly damaged tree-shaded Madan temple. His name is Sappani Madan. I have never seen him. He stands there as an irregular stone. The woman who raised me used to tell me stories about him before I slept.

Once you cross Keezhamaruva stream, it is someone else’s domain. His name is Eenthadi Madan. In Kattoor it is Sappani Madan! In Karumbanur it is Eenthadi Madan! This Eenthadi Madan has a younger one! His name is Nochiyadi Madan! On the Ambasamudram-Alangulam road, near Alangulam, in Kaalathimadam, he stands under a neem tree. Every time I go to Chennai, the Gandhavel mini-bus passes through Kattoor-Karumbanur-Kaalathimadam and joins the Ambai-Alangulam road. At that junction, Nochiyadi Madan, with bulging eyes and unsmiling face, sees me off every single time. Nochiyadi Madan is a bit well-off. Sappani Madan under the neem in Vembadi has black stone! Eenthadi Madan surrounded by palms has a pedestal! Nochiyadi Madan surrounded by neem has a carved stone statue! A perfectly built temple!

Going near Sappani Madan happens only occasionally! One day when the whole village collects money and sacrifices a goat! One day when a single family goes alone to offer a chicken sacrifice! In childhood, if we went with those doing chicken sacrifice, we were sure to get grilled intestines. After twisting the chicken’s neck, my cousins and I would sit and pluck the feathers. They were experts at cutting it. After washing the intestines clean, we would pile up dry leaves, light them, and roast the intestines! We would try to offer it to Sappani Madan. With great generosity, he would give it all back to us. We would measure the length of the roasted, curled intestine and divide it according to who worked more! Still, if one person finished eating quickly, the one still eating would feed the other. It was the custom! What a taste that was! My mouth is watering just thinking of it! There is something even tastier than roasted intestine! That is roasted “kallaali”! I will write about that another day!

After crossing Keezhamaruva stream, you have to walk 700m over rocky ground to reach the banyan tree! If you walk carelessly, your foot slips and teeth break. That is certain; some friends who came to see me can bear witness! Otherwise, you have to take a slightly longer route! Here is Saamiyaar Kulam, the pond where I first learned to swim. In rainy seasons, many springs open and the pond collects pure water. I have seen big snakes in this pond many times. Once I jumped in and saved a struggling peacock chick. I even took mud from this pond to make an Amman idol. In dry seasons, I have lain in the lap of this pond, staring at the sky and slept. When I reached the banyan tree, a small smile came naturally! Is this banyan tree not the one that read many pages of my life’s book!

Lying under, no, in the lap of, the banyan tree, I looked up at the sky! Even though the leaves and branches hid me when I cried, the face of the woman who raised me crossed over them all and appeared! Tears came, and a small breeze arrived to console. When I turned in the direction the breeze came from, something widened the pupils of my eyes!

I shouted! I jumped up! In summer, avaram bushes burst into yellow flowers and laugh! Likewise, in rainy season, these red ginger lily plants emerge from their rhizomes toward sunlight. Though completely poisonous, I have such fascination for this creeper! It grows in wet soil! It blooms on the Eelam creeper! This fiery-blooming kaandal, after a small pat of encouragement, I hurried to see Eenthadi Madan.

There is another small unnamed stream. Since it is completely wet soil, kolunji plants peek out here and there; I stepped carefully. The calm banyan tree! Under it, four or five platforms! On each, small remnants of community feasts! For all of them, Eenthadi Madan is the master! In that place, he is the king! When entering, a memory from many years ago came: early morning, coming out of that temple with mental agony, an old man who saw me and got startled, dropped his bicycle, and ran to bring me palm toddy. Thinking of that brought a gentle smile. Smiling, as I approached Madan, it felt as if he was saying, “I’ve been watching you like a dot on the forehead for so long, standing here! Are you going to the city, boy!” At that moment, I should have played Raja’s line, “Wherever I go, all my thoughts are right here,” and shown it to Madan. Instead, stunned, I patted Madan a little and moved on!

Nearby is Karumbanur’s cremation ground! There too there are banyan trees! Even an adhipambu! If boiled and steamed, it becomes something! If you sit at the nearby burning platform station, the silence there cannot be found anywhere else. The still banyan leaves, what are they trying to say? Why does the wind blowing everywhere keep silence only here? Is it to avoid disturbing the sleepers? Or has it become subdued after hearing so many wails!? Slowly leaving that place, plucking some soondu grass at the entrance, chewing it, I walked on.

Soondu grass is equivalent to lime grass. In Karthigai, along with sokka palm, they burn together like soda palm! They are tasty! They give fragrance to the mouth! Slowly they make saliva flow. I chewed in the evening; I tasted. Even now (1:47 am), the taste hasn’t left my tongue. Chewing it while walking, for some reason, a frenzy makes you walk faster! It is a small joy of imagining oneself as a hero in that deserted world! I walked fast! In childhood, while coming back from school on a bicycle, in the empty road hours, craving this petty pleasure, I would ride fast, then swerve here and there, and then at the place called Siluvai Modangi, crash into a rock and dislocate my knee.

But today what happened was a little different. Who could match my speed! The chewing grass makes me bounce! If kalli trees block the way, so what!? Are they my rivals? I pushed them aside and walked further. As soon as I entered a small dense area, I controlled my speed. Because on my right side there was a burial pit! Burial pit meaning not loose sand or mud! It is the place where someone was buried! To mark it and to prevent the smell of the buried body from escaping, they slightly raise that spot, plaster it with lime, and place a small pot (kaalai) upside down at the head end, also plastered with lime. Near it, they grow pirandai. The woman who raised me said that just as pirandai spreads, the lineage of the deceased will spread too.

Seeing the burial pit, my legs stopped. But something was not right! I looked down! I was standing on a burial pit too! But an old one from many days ago! Without pirandai, only patches of long-ago applied lime visible. Immediately I got down, at its head end said “Maappu koduthurunga” (forgive me), and in my usual gait went to another banyan tree. This tree is good for playing. The hanging roots here are strong. Because that unnamed stream embraces it, you have to climb and descend its bank.

However, just a little before that, there is a small well. If I go home without peering into it, sleep would evade me and I would have to wait for another night. So I inspected it. As soon as my head appeared, one yellow-striped snake quickly hid among the thorns; another one, unmindful of me, crawled and played for a long time. Enjoying that, my evening turned into night. Climbing up and down the stream, tapping the banyan roots, I left that place and walked toward home.

Itching started on both legs. Even though I know well why, according to the Baader-Meinhof Effect, this mind prefers to attribute it to whatever it unknowingly stepped on. The battle between me and my mind happens from time to time. At those moments it becomes clear to me that I am not the mind. This is a kind of humor; not spirituality.

Sandatti leaves and nayuruvi plants, every time I return home, caress me asking “Are you leaving us?” That caress, though out of love, the result of their nature is this itching. When walking past them, for every poke of nerunchi, no pain matches it. That was my leg without footwear time; this is my computer work time. So to nerunchi, it is only a small disappointment.

Again, when crossing Keezhamaruva and entering the village, if I turn back a little, there stands my forest, ready for the night, adorned with silence! The sound of karuvaali, the calls of quails, the kalli forest that shows! The sound of guiding calls, the screeching of arinonanjans, the beautiful forest that unfolds! The dark forest that is home to Eenthadi Madan and the wet kaandal! The forest where monitor lizards, peacocks, porcupines, and spiny mice sprout!

The small light these memories give will keep mental fatigue away and shine brightly until my next visit!
